Chapter 4 Stability Analysis

Lemma 4.1 (Barbalat's Lemma) Let \(f(t)\) be differentiable, if

  • \(\lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} f(t)\) is finite, and

  • \(\dot{f}(t)\) is uniformly continuous,3

then \[ \lim_{t \rightarrow \infty} \dot{f}(t) = 0. \]

Theorem 4.1 (Barbalat's Stability Certificate) If a scalar function \(V(x,t)\) satisfies

  • \(V(x,t)\) is lower bounded,

  • \(\dot{V}(x,t)\) is negative semidefinite

  • \(\dot{V}(x,t)\) is uniformly continuous

then \(\dot{V}(x,t) \rightarrow 0\) as \(t \rightarrow \infty\).

Proof. \(V(x,t)\) is lower bounded and \(\dot{V}\) is negative semidefinite implies the limit of \(V\) as \(t \rightarrow \infty\) is finite (note that \(V(x,t) \leq V(x(0),0)\)). Then the theorem clearly follows from Barbalat’s Lemma 4.1.

  1. A sufficient condition for this to hold is that \(\ddot{f}\) exists and is bounded.↩︎
